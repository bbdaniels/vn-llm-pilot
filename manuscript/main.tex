\title{Large Language Models for Clinical Vignette Assessment:\\A Pilot and Validation Study in Vietnam}
\author{Benjamin Daniels, Wenjia(Stella) Zhang, Hoang Nguyen, and David Duong}

\date{\today}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
\noindent We developed and validated a self-administered clinical vignette platform powered by a large language model (LLM), deployed through SurveyCTO, to measure primary health care provider competencies in Vietnam. In a pilot focus group, nine physicians rated LLM-simulated patient interactions as realistic (mean 3.78/5) and user-friendly. In the validation phase, 22 providers completed 132 vignette interactions across ten clinical scenarios. Essential diagnostic checklist scores -- human-coded from transcripts -- correlated with expert clinician evaluations (Pearson's $\rho$ = 0.49--0.59). LLM-automated coding of checklist items from transcripts was feasible but noisy relative to human coding ($\rho$ = 0.35). Multiple-choice knowledge test scores showed no significant correlation with vignette performance, suggesting the tool measures distinct dimensions of competency. The total cost of 132 interactions was under USD 2. LLM-driven conversational vignettes represent a valid, low-cost, and scalable method for assessing provider competencies, eliminating the need for enumerators while preserving the open-ended format critical to vignette validity.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Human resources for health -- including investments in medical education as well as the salaries of health care providers in both the public and private sectors -- represent the majority of total health expenditures in most countries \citep{ranson2010priorities}. However, it is not clear that investments in primary health care education and training are made efficiently or cost-effectively, especially in resource-constrained settings \citep{daniels2024medical}. For many common ailments, research has demonstrated low quality primary care for both infectious and non-communicable diseases, regardless of the type of health system financing or organization in place \citep{das2023prices, banerjee2023market}. A range of investments in various types of provider training and education programs have proven marginally effective at best, and most such programs are time-intensive, costly, and unlikely to translate to new contexts and environments \citep{rowe2018effectiveness, lilford2025policy}.

Large-scale studies have used clinical vignettes to map human resources readiness across health systems and to investigate health care provider competencies in diagnosis and treatment tasks, which provide a means to evaluate quality-improvement interventions \citep{daniels2024medical, andrews2021quality, das2022two}. By eliciting each provider's approach to the diagnosis and management of simulated primary outpatient presentations for common tracer conditions, such vignettes measure the best possible standard of care that providers are capable of delivering \citep{das2005which, das2007money, leonard2005use}. Traditional vignettes surveys typically use ``roleplay-style'' approaches, where one enumerator plays the part of a patient while the provider conducts an open-ended investigation. For each clinical scenario, the enumerator gives a short complaint -- for (undiagnosed) pulmonary tuberculosis they might say, ``Hello doctor, my name is Toan, 40 years old. I have had a cough for several weeks that has not gone away, I always feel tired.'' The provider is prompted to take history and conduct clinical examinations. When asked about weight loss, the enumerator might respond, ``I think I have lost weight because my clothes are looser now, but it could also be because it is hot and I do not have much appetite.'' The enumerator might also say ``Heart examination was normal, no abnormal heart sounds or signs of heart failure,'' if the provider indicated the examination.

To encode structured data, a second enumerator marks off questions from a pre-determined ``essential diagnostic checklist'' of relevant questions and examinations that the provider could ask, reveals the results of exams and labs when ordered by the provider, and records the provider's final diagnosis and initial care plan. Although it is well established that there is a `know-do gap' between clinical competencies measured in this testing environment and actual service delivery practices that may depend, among other factors, on resourcing at health facilities \citep{ibnat2019three}, these types of clinical competency measures have emerged as a core measurement framework for progress towards Universal Health Coverage (UHC) goals \citep{who2022global}. For pre-specified key questions and exams, (non)completion allows for quantitative comparisons of diagnostic knowledge across providers using well-validated psychometric methods like item response theory \citep{daniels2024medical, das2005which, das2007money}. After the provider indicated they are done with the history taking portion, including ordering relevant lab tests, they are prompted to give a diagnosis and treatment plan.

Vignettes surveys, however, are a challenge to conduct at large scales or frequent intervals for rapid program evaluation. This is primarily because there is currently no low-cost, scalable way to have vignettes be remotely self-administered by providers. For example, it is not appropriate to utilize a digital ``multiple choice'' survey offering providers a (searchable) list of all possible questions, exams, laboratory tests, and treatments to select from, as unprompted elicitation of knowledge is the foundation of the method's validity. To be valid, the vignettes method requires that providers be able to lead open-ended interactions with the ``patient'', and that their actions are recognized and categorized by the enumerator into specific categories so that appropriate clinical information can be given in response and translated to well-defined data structures after the completion of the interaction. To achieve this, large-scale programs such as the World Bank's decade-long Service Delivery Indicators \citep{andrews2021quality} typically spend large budget shares on enumerator travel, compensation, and expenses. As a result, such surveys offer only static snapshots of provider competencies that occur infrequently and require substantial time investments in project funding, start-up, and one-time analysis.

This study approached these limitations with a novel technology of self-administered and automatically-coded clinical vignettes. We developed an LLM-based ``chatbot'' interface \citep{geiecke2024conversations} using the web-based SurveyCTO survey software (including mobile phone compatibility). In Vietnam, we piloted and validated a web-based, open-source, scalable, flexible, and self-administered medical vignette survey tool powered by the OpenAI API (chatgpt-nano-4.1). We also piloted an automated feature extraction system using the same LLM to classify provider performance from the resulting transcripts. Expert clinicians developed 10 clinical cases -- five common primary care conditions and five hepatitis-related -- detailing each virtual patient's medical and socioeconomic background for prompting into the system. The LLM tool was able to successfully role-play these scenarios in respondents' language of choice with a single prompt, via mobile phone connection on respondents' personal devices. The presentation was judged realistic by respondents; it operated at a low cost; and it seamlessly integrated into the SurveyCTO platform. Once the interaction transcripts were captured, the second LLM was able to transform them into structured data matching a pre-specified checklist data schema, which specified the history-taking questions, clinical exams, and laboratory tests considered to be ``essential diagnostics'' and the ``correct'' treatment protocol.

The in-person focus group of 9 providers completed 49 interactions. They provided feedback on the time burden, clinical realism, and user interface, which we describe here. We then did so with a validation sample of 22 providers, who completed a total of 132 vignettes interactions. Our results from the focus group sample demonstrated that the methodology was feasible to deploy without any face-to-face support. Using the validation sample data, we compared the competency metrics obtained from human-coding of the transcripts; from holistic evaluation of the transcripts by the expert clinicians who had designed the scenarios (one in the original Vietnamese and one in English); from LLM-automated coding of the essential diagnostics checklist; and against an external multiple choice clinical knowledge test that used the same hepatitis scenarios as a basis.

Our results demonstrated that automated LLM-based data extraction from transcripts was feasible; and that competency measures correlated well across LLM coding, human coding, and expert grading in English and in the original language. However, the LLM-graded measures were statistically noisy relative to the human-coded ground truth measures, and would require substantial refinement to be valid as competency measures at the individual level. Furthermore, we found no statistically significant association between the vignettes competency measures and the knowledge tests, suggesting that the diagnostic competency measures a different domain of clinical knowledge. We concluded that the conversational vignettes LLM method has the potential to produce a low-cost, flexible, and scalable platform to be adapted to a wide variety of contexts and support the rapid, valid measure of health care provider competencies worldwide.

\section{Materials and Methods}

\subsection{Study design and participants}

In August 2025, we administered a web-based SurveyCTO survey (either by laptop or by mobile phone) that included LLM-based virtual patient ``chatbots'' preloaded with a pool of ten different clinical cases (five generalist and five hepatitis-related) (\textbf{Table 1}). Each case scenario -- detailing the patient's socioeconomic background, chief complaint, medical history, clinical exam results, and potential responses to a set of ``checklist'' questions -- was scripted by expert clinicians supporting the research team. The OpenAI LLM API was then prompted through the software to perform according to these scenarios such that reasonable standardization was achieved across interactions. The scenarios developed for this study are attached in the \textbf{Supplemental Appendix}.

The research team first recruited and consented 9 physicians from a Provincial Hospital in Phu Tho, Vietnam, to a focus group for usability testing and UI/UX refinement. These providers received a live demonstration by the research team and were instructed to complete as many case scenarios as they found reasonable in a 45-minute period, followed by closed-ended (Likert scale) feedback questions on the realism of each case presentation. Two days later, we conducted a structured group discussion to collect open-ended feedback, which we incorporated into the validation round.

For the validation sample, the research team contacted and consented 22 providers who were enrolled in the 2024 cohort of the StITCH hepatitis care training program, which co-designed and delivered a people-centered model of care for hepatitis B and C at the primary health care level in Vietnam. The validation group was given a Zoom orientation with no additional in-person support. These providers were assigned to complete six vignettes selected at random (three general cases and three hepatitis cases each). Afterwards, they completed a traditional multiple-choice knowledge test on hepatitis care, which included clinical knowledge questions based on the same five hepatitis scenarios that could have appeared in the vignettes survey. Although the total number of providers included in the validation sample was small, they completed 132 interactions containing 433 unique data points, allowing sufficient independent variation to assess the relationships between the performance of various measures, given that we do not engage in statistical hypothesis testing.

\subsection{Outcomes}

For the focus group, primary outcomes were: (1) qualitative feedback on the LLM-based vignettes survey obtained through the FGD, and (2) quantitative ratings of scenario presentation realism. For the validation sample, the primary outcome was validation of the human-coded ``essential diagnostics checklist'' LLM vignette score via interaction-level correlations with: (1) expert clinician grading of the transcripts both in English and in the original Vietnamese; and (2) the hepatitis multiple choice knowledge test scores for matched cases. Our secondary outcome was the reliability of the LLM-coded ``essential diagnostics checklist'', assessed by correlation with ground truth from human-coding.

\subsection{Data analysis}

For the focus group, the FGD transcript was thematically analyzed by the research team to identify common themes for tool refinement and future development. Provider perceptions of vignette realism were captured through structured survey questions (``How would you rate the overall realism of this simulated patient?'', ``Given the final diagnosis, how would you rate the realism of the clinical presentation?'', and ``How would you rate the realism of the social and economic presentation?''). We calculated means and 95\% confidence intervals for each.

For the validation sample, transcripts were translated to English using Google Translate and manually coded by the research team to determine whether each provider: (1) asked each of the pre-specified checklist history-taking questions, (2) ordered the recommended clinical examinations and laboratory tests, and (3) offered the correct diagnosis. The transcripts were also coded using the OpenAI API using an identical schema as the manual coding, asking for each item, ``In the following text, if the user was a doctor, did they specifically [ask/order/prescribe]\ldots''. Using the human-coded and LLM-coded checklist items separately, a ``checklist score'' was calculated for each vignette response.

Agreement between manual and automated data extraction was assessed by calculating the proportion of agreement, true positives, false positives, true negatives, and false negatives based on the human coding as ground truth. The two expert clinicians who developed the vignettes also scored provider performance on each transcript from 1--5 separately for history-taking, diagnosis accuracy, and management completeness (one using the Vietnamese original and one using the English translation). The hepatitis knowledge test performance was scored as the total number of correct responses out of the administered items. Agreement of the human grading with each of these measures was also assessed through unadjusted ordinary least squares (OLS) regression. Clustering was not necessary in any case since we calculated results separately by scenario -- no provider repeated the same scenario twice.

\subsection{Ethical approval}

All procedures were approved by the ethical review board of the Hanoi University of Public Health (025-375/DD-YTCC) and Mass General Brigham Institutional Review Board (Protocol Number 2023P003011).

\section{Results}

\subsection{Focus Group}

Seven out of the nine providers completed six cases within the allotted 45-minutes focus group session, with average interaction time ranging from 3.4 to 9.2 minutes per case. All interactions were conducted in Vietnamese on smartphones. Across all conditions, respondents rated overall case realism positively, an average 3.78/5 (95\% CI: 3.65--3.9).

Qualitative feedback highlighted several notable strengths. Providers consistently emphasized the ease of use and user-friendliness of the LLM-based conversational vignettes: \textit{``Compared to other research tools, this one has a simpler interface and is easier to operate.''} Fast and contextually appropriate responses were the most frequently cited benefits (4 mentions), with providers noting that information provided was sufficient and meaningful for clinical decision-making: \textit{``The virtual patient's responses are relatively accurate, realistic, and easy to work with.''} Others also commended its realism, such as language localization and the ability to return to history-taking after laboratory results were delivered: \textit{``The language has been fully localized into Vietnamese and very understandable''}; \textit{``It doesn't give us everything at once for us to guess. The more we ask, [\ldots] the more accurate the diagnosis will be.''} Another physician summarized: \textit{``Regarding the system's responses [\ldots], the level of realism is good. If we guide or structure our questions clearly, it will deliver as expected.''}

A few respondents noted minor issues that affected the interactions. The most common limitation was that certain laboratory or imaging tests were not performed or had results omitted (4 mentions). \textit{``Some para-clinical tests weren't done, but when I asked a few related questions, [the responses] felt complete.''} Although the limited test results raised concerns regarding realism, this was an intentional design feature to ensure reasonable standardization of the virtual patients. Tests deemed irrelevant for the case were prompted to have results reported as ``normal'' or ``unremarkable''. The absence of non-verbal cues was also considered a barrier to realistic clinical interaction: \textit{``When we see patients in person, we can quickly evaluate whether there is an infectious syndrome. For asthma patients, just by observing their breathing we can tell how severe their respiratory distress is. But in a simulation, we are limited in the questions we can ask.''}

Respondents recommended several enhancements to strengthen the tool for the future. Suggestions include integrating visual elements such as laboratory images and video patient profiles, adding automatic feedback on performance, and eventually transitioning from a web-based platform to an independent, offline smartphone application. One respondent also mentioned the potential benefit of including more complicated case scenarios to match clinical reality: \textit{``In real hospitals, most patients don't just have one disease -- they usually have multiple comorbidities.''} Overall, the focus group participants suggested that the LLM-based vignettes survey was both usable and realistic.

\subsection{Validation Sample}

Twenty-two providers completed 132 interactions in the validation sample. The average interaction lasted 10.19 minutes (95\% CI: 9.03--11.35), excluding outliers lasting longer than one hour (\textbf{Table 2}). The typical interaction resulted in the completion of 18.66\% of the essential diagnostics checklist items for that case scenario (95\% CI: 16.56--20.75). Based on the Vietnamese transcripts, the first clinical expert rated the average interaction 3.42 on a 1--5 scale (95\% CI: 3.24--3.59); and based on the English transcripts, the other expert rated the average interaction 3.21 (95\% CI: 3.05--3.37). The two expert ratings agreed well, with some variation (Pearson's $\rho$ = 0.62, OLS $r^{2}$ = 0.38). Compared with the human-coded score on the essential diagnostics list, the expert ratings had Pearson's $\rho$ = 0.49 and 0.59 for Vietnamese and English, respectively. In regression, a 10 percentage point (p.p.) increase in the checklist completion corresponded with an approximate 0.45-point increase in the raters' grading. \textbf{Figure \ref{fig:validation}} illustrates these correlations.

The LLM-automated coding was less strongly correlated with the human coding compared to the expert evaluations, and had substantially more statistical noise (Pearson's $\rho$ = 0.35; OLS $r^{2}$ = 0.12). The correlation was statistically significant in regression against the human ground truth, with a 10p.p. increase in the human-coded essential diagnostics checklist predicting a 1.7p.p. increase in the LLM-coded checklist (\textbf{Figure \ref{fig:validation}, Panel C}). Finally, the multiple choice question (MCQ) knowledge score for the matched hepatitis had no statistically significant correlation with the essential diagnostics completion for the same cases (Pearson's $\rho$ = 0.07; OLS $r^{2}$ $<$ 0.01; \textbf{Figure \ref{fig:validation}, Panel D}).

The accuracy of the LLM coding of the essential diagnostics items varied widely. \textbf{Figure \ref{fig:agreement}} plots the sensitivity and specificity of the LLM coding for 304 items for which both were calculable. The mean sensitivity was 0.31 (95\% CI: 0.26--0.35); the mean specificity was 0.85 (95\% CI: 0.83--0.87). There were three major clusters of items to consider. First, there were many items with zero sensitivity; most of which have very few positives in the data. A large cluster of those had perfect specificity, indicating that the LLM accurately predicted ``No'' for these items each time. Another cluster had perfect sensitivity, and these varied widely in specificity. Notably, among the rest of the items, most were above the line of unity.

\section{Discussion}

Evidence from a range of low- and middle-income countries suggests that the quality of medical education and training has not persistently improved over the last few decades. For example, in a sample of providers from 11 sub-Saharan African countries, \citet{daniels2024medical} estimated that, ``under current patterns [of improvement in medical education], it would require 250 years of new provider cohorts to increase average correct management by 10 [percentage points] above current levels''. Across the sub-Saharan African sample, the study found surprisingly small and variable differences in medical competencies across providers with different levels of formal training, demonstrating inconsistent value-for-money in medical education in these settings. Similar patterns have been observed in India, where medical competencies among untrained informal providers closely tracked that of formally educated providers across states, and where -- despite the official existence of national minimum standards of formal medical education -- the competencies of medical graduates varied dramatically across different levels of socioeconomic development \citep{das2022two}.

Asian nations share these challenges of developing consistent and competent human resources for health. Despite large population bases and growing investments in medical education, studies from China, India, Indonesia, and across Southeast Asia have shown that delivered care quality remains low and continues to face barriers to improvement even in the post-COVID-19 era \citep{das2022two, sassi2024impact, zhang2024quality, harrison2015patient}. Vietnam -- a country of 100 million people with a per-capita GDP of US\$4,282 -- is no exception \citep{demombynes2016quality, zawahir2021standardised}. Despite major investments in a government-run primary care system financed by social health insurance, care-seekers often choose to bypass this system and present at private providers, tertiary facilities, and pharmacies instead, incurring substantial out-of-pocket costs. As a result, Vietnam's investments have so far faced challenges in delivering the goals of high-quality comprehensive care and financial protection for its population. The most recent comprehensive assessment of the quality and availability of primary care in Vietnam, using a range of common conditions as indicators, was conducted in 2015 \citep{demombynes2016quality}. Due to the expense of replicating such an effort, there has not been a large-scale post-COVID-19 evaluation of the competencies of medical professionals at undergraduate medical programs or health care facilities.

The clinical vignettes method that informed these studies relies on an open-ended interaction format to assess the competencies of medical providers. Training and deploying vignettes enumerators to work across large geographies and large workforces is a resource-intensive investment, meaning new tools are now needed to enable continuous evaluation of provider competencies in an actionable and operationalizable fashion. Our results indicated that the measures of competency derived from medical vignettes were valid in this method and correlated well with expert evaluations of performance. When compared to clinical knowledge tests in matched hepatitis conditions, we found that the vignettes performance measured a different dimension of clinical competency (Pearson's $\rho$ = 0.07; OLS $r^{2}$ $<$ 0.01), which we consider to potentially capture a diagnostic competency as opposed to a treatment competency. Future directions for tool refinement may include incorporating video and audio profiles for simulated patients to enhance realism through non-verbal cues. The language and tone of the LLM simulated patients can also be further refined using detailed system prompting and training the LLM model with validation data.

We found that an additional OpenAI API LLM prompt was able to extract structured data to our specifications directly from the transcripts, and that these data correlated weakly (Pearson's $\rho$ = 0.35; OLS $r^{2}$ = 0.12) with the data produced by human coding. While computer-assisted (Google) translation and human data extraction was not excessively burdensome at this scale, further research on the reliability and cost of automated data extraction may streamline data encoding at larger scales. Automated data extraction also allowed the preservation of more complete data in the form of transcripts and could enable future refinement or modification of checklists to fit the research purpose, rather than pre-encoding the data structure. Future directions for research might also include the assessment of more expensive models, such as modern ``reasoning'' models; or develop a multi-shot ``voting'' algorithm to overcome some of its variability. This study therefore demonstrates and provides a platform for implementation of conversational clinical vignettes measuring medical competencies in a digital, scalable, and flexible setting.
